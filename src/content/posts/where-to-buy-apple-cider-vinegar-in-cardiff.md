## What is the mother, and why does it matter?

The mother is the most important thing to look for on the label, and the easiest thing to overlook.

It's a naturally occurring colony of proteins, enzymes, and beneficial bacteria that forms during fermentation. You can see it: a cloudy, slightly stringy sediment at the bottom of the bottle. Raw, unfiltered vinegar contains it. Pasteurised, filtered vinegar does not.

Most supermarket apple cider vinegar has been heated and filtered to look clear and shelf-friendly. That process removes the mother entirely. If the bottle is perfectly clear, the mother is gone.

Bragg's is raw and unfiltered. The mother is very much in there. Shake before use.

## Why raw and organic?

Raw means it hasn't been pasteurised. Organic means the apples were grown without synthetic pesticides and the vinegar hasn't been processed with anything artificial. For a product you're likely taking daily, both matter.

Bragg's is USDA certified organic and has been since well before organic certification was fashionable.

## UK raw organic alternatives worth knowing about

Bragg's is imported from the US, which is fine, but there are excellent British and European alternatives worth knowing about.

**Willy's ACV** is the most notable UK option. Made on a 300-year-old organic farm in Herefordshire from 100% British apples, raw, unpasteurised, and with the live mother. If you want to buy British, this is the one.

**Raw Health** is a well-established UK health food brand producing organic, unfiltered ACV that's never been heated or pasteurised. Straightforward, reliable, and widely respected in independent health food retail.

**Clearspring** takes a slightly different route: their ACV is made in Italy by an artisan producer using cold-pressed organic apple juice, naturally fermented and unfiltered. A good option if you prefer a European origin.

All three have the mother. All three are raw and organic. The differences are largely about origin and flavour profile rather than quality.

## How do people actually use it?

The most common uses we hear about in store:

- A tablespoon in a glass of water first thing in the morning, sometimes with a little honey or lemon
- As a salad dressing base, on its own or with olive oil and mustard
- Stirred into warm water with ginger as a daily tonic
- As a natural hair rinse diluted with water

The morning drink is by far the most popular. If you're new to it, start with less than you think you need. It's acidic and takes some getting used to.

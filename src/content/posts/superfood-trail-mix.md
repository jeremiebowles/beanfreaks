Trail mix is one of the most practical snacks to have around. Done well, it is high in protein, healthy fats, and slow-releasing energy. Done badly, it is mostly sugar. Here is how to make a version that earns the "superfood" label.

## The base: nuts

Nuts provide the foundation of a good trail mix. They are calorie-dense, high in healthy fats, and carry enough protein and fibre to make the snack genuinely sustaining.

**Almonds** are a solid all-purpose choice. High in vitamin E, magnesium, and protein. They hold their texture well and do not go soft quickly.

**Cashews** are slightly softer and creamier. Lower in fat than almonds but higher in zinc and iron, which makes them a good choice for plant-based diets.

**Walnuts** are the highest plant-based source of ALA omega-3 fatty acids. They go slightly rancid faster than other nuts so buy in smaller quantities and keep the mix in an airtight container.

**Brazil nuts** contain selenium in amounts that most other foods cannot match. One or two a day provides a meaningful contribution to daily intake. More than a small handful regularly is not recommended.

Aim for a roughly equal mix or weight whichever you prefer and eat most. Buying raw, unsalted nuts keeps the overall sodium down and preserves more of the natural fats.

## Seeds

Seeds add a different texture, extra micronutrients, and concentrated nutrition in a small volume.

**Pumpkin seeds** are high in zinc, magnesium, and iron. They have a satisfying chew and a mild flavour that works well with everything else. Good for immune function and energy.

**Sunflower seeds** are high in vitamin E and selenium. Lighter than pumpkin seeds and slightly nutty.

**Hemp seeds** are one of the few complete plant proteins, containing all nine essential amino acids. They have a soft texture and a gentle, slightly nutty flavour. They blend into the mix without dominating it.

## Dried fruit

Dried fruit adds natural sweetness and quick-release energy. The key is keeping the proportion lower than the nuts and seeds so the sugar content stays balanced.

**Goji berries** are the classic superfood addition. High in antioxidants, vitamin C, and zeaxanthin, which supports eye health. They have a slightly tart, cranberry-like flavour.

**Dried mulberries** are sweeter and softer, with a good iron content for a fruit. They make a good alternative to raisins with a more interesting flavour.

**Apricots** add chewiness and beta-carotene. Go for unsulphured apricots if you can: the brown ones rather than the bright orange ones. Sulphur dioxide used to preserve the colour is a common allergen and the unsulphured versions tend to have a richer, more concentrated flavour.

Avoid dried fruit coated in oil or added sugar. Check the ingredients: it should be just the fruit.

## The extras

**Cacao nibs** add a dark, bitter chocolate note without any added sugar. They contain iron, magnesium, and flavanols. A small amount goes a long way.

**Coconut flakes** add sweetness and a light crunch. Go for unsweetened. They add a tropical note that works particularly well with dried mango if you want to take the mix in that direction.

**Dark chocolate chips** are fine in moderation. Go for at least 70% cocoa content. The darker the chocolate, the lower the sugar and the higher the flavanols.

## A simple recipe

This makes enough to fill a 500ml jar:

- 100g mixed nuts (almonds, cashews, walnuts)
- 50g pumpkin seeds
- 30g sunflower seeds
- 20g hemp seeds
- 40g goji berries
- 30g dried mulberries or apricots (chopped)
- 20g cacao nibs

Mix everything together and store in an airtight container. Keeps well at room temperature for two to three weeks, longer in the fridge.
